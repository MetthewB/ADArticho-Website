The New Yorker is Hiring!

The New Yorker, a prestigious magazine known for its witty and thought-provoking content, is on the hunt for a new editorialist. The HR director is overwhelmed with applications from humorists worldwide, each claiming to have the perfect sense of humor to entertain the magazine's readers.

But how can the HR director choose the right candidate? Humor is subjective, and what makes one person laugh might not resonate with another. To solve this problem, we’ve analyzed two datasets: one from The New Yorker and another from Oxford. These datasets provide insights into what makes a joke funny, how humor evolves over time, and what themes resonate most with readers.

Using these insights, we’ve created a quiz to determine if you have what it takes to match the humor profile of New Yorker readers. Are you ready to prove your comedic prowess?

How Does Humor Vary Across Cultures?

Humor is deeply influenced by cultural norms and traditions. What’s funny in one culture might not resonate in another. For example, wordplay and puns are highly appreciated in some cultures, while slapstick humor dominates in others.

Our analysis shows that cultural references in jokes often determine their appeal to specific audiences.

The Importance of Timing in Humor

Timing is everything in comedy. A well-timed punchline can turn an average joke into a hilarious one. Our analysis shows that jokes with a delayed punchline or an unexpected twist tend to perform better.

However, delivering the punchline too early or too late can ruin the joke entirely.

Research Questions

  • RQ1 – Thematic Structure: What aspects of their lives do people denounce or joke about most often? Which themes and types of humor recur most frequently in humorous captions?
  • RQ2 – Cultural Bubble: Do the successful captions in the New Yorker dataset exhibit cultural or thematic biases that differ from those in the Oxford dataset?
  • RQ3 – Temporal Change: How do the thematic structures of humor evolve over time, and can major sociopolitical or cultural events be linked to shifts in the popularity of certain humor themes?

Exploratory Data Analysis

Our analysis revealed key insights into humor distribution and voting patterns:

  • Most captions in both datasets are rated as "Not Funny," with only a small subset receiving high funniness scores.
  • The New Yorker dataset shows a more balanced distribution of captions per image compared to the Oxford dataset.
  • Humor perception follows a power-law distribution: a few captions dominate the upper tail, while most receive minimal engagement.

These findings highlight the subjective nature of humor and the challenges of analyzing it quantitatively.

Humor Classification

We classified captions into humor types using two approaches:

  • Zero-Shot Classification: Using a pre-trained language model (BART) to assign humor types based on predefined categories.
  • Sentence Embeddings: Using sentence-transformers to generate embeddings and cluster captions by humor type.

These methods provide complementary insights into the thematic and stylistic dimensions of humor.
