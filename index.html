The Laughter Lens

A data-driven exploration of humor across institutional (The New Yorker) and general (Oxford) contexts.

Research Questions

  • RQ1 – Thematic Structure: What aspects of their lives do people denounce or joke about most often? Which themes and types of humor recur most frequently in humorous captions?
  • RQ2 – Cultural Bubble: Do the successful captions in the New Yorker dataset exhibit cultural or thematic biases that differ from those in the Oxford dataset?
  • RQ3 – Temporal Change: How do the thematic structures of humor evolve over time, and can major sociopolitical or cultural events be linked to shifts in the popularity of certain humor themes?

Exploratory Data Analysis

Our analysis revealed key insights into humor distribution and voting patterns:

  • Most captions in both datasets are rated as "Not Funny," with only a small subset receiving high funniness scores.
  • The New Yorker dataset shows a more balanced distribution of captions per image compared to the Oxford dataset.
  • Humor perception follows a power-law distribution: a few captions dominate the upper tail, while most receive minimal engagement.

These findings highlight the subjective nature of humor and the challenges of analyzing it quantitatively.

Humor Classification

We classified captions into humor types using two approaches:

  • Zero-Shot Classification: Using a pre-trained language model (BART) to assign humor types based on predefined categories.
  • Sentence Embeddings: Using sentence-transformers to generate embeddings and cluster captions by humor type.

These methods provide complementary insights into the thematic and stylistic dimensions of humor.
